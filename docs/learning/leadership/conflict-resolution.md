## Overview

Put a group of passionate people under deadline pressure and conflict is
inevitable — over designs, priorities, workload, or just personalities. Conflict
itself isn't bad; **unmanaged** conflict is. Handled well, disagreement produces
better decisions and stronger relationships. This lesson gives you a calm,
repeatable way to work through it.

## Prerequisites

- [Communication Fundamentals](../communication-fundamentals/) and [Giving & Receiving Feedback](../feedback/).

## What you'll learn

- The difference between healthy and unhealthy conflict.
- A step-by-step method for resolving a disagreement between two people.
- How to mediate a conflict between teammates.
- How to keep conflict about ideas, not people.

## Unit 1 — Healthy vs. unhealthy conflict

- **Healthy conflict** is about *ideas* — debating which design is better. It's
  open, respectful, and makes the decision stronger.
- **Unhealthy conflict** is about *people* — personal attacks, grudges, silent
  resentment, or avoidance.

Your job as a leader is to **encourage the first and defuse the second**. A team
with no disagreement isn't harmonious — it's usually just unsafe to speak up.

## Unit 2 — Address it early

Small tensions left alone grow into big ones. When you notice friction:

- **Don't avoid it** hoping it resolves itself — it rarely does.
- **Address it privately** and promptly, before it spreads to the group.
- **Assume good intent** — most conflict comes from misunderstanding or pressure,
  not malice.

## Unit 3 — A resolution method

When you're in a disagreement:

1. **Cool down** if emotions are high — never resolve mid-blowup.
2. **Find shared ground** — you both want the robot to work. Name the common goal.
3. **Separate people from the problem** — attack the issue, not each other.
4. **Understand before persuading** — each side states their view; the other
   reflects it back until both feel heard.
5. **Focus on interests, not positions** — ask *why* each side wants what they
   want; the real need is often compatible.
6. **Generate options** together and agree on a path, plus how you'll check it
   worked.

## Unit 4 — Mediating between teammates

When two teammates are stuck:

- Get them talking **to each other**, not through you.
- Stay **neutral**; your job is the process, not picking a winner.
- Enforce **ground rules**: one person talks at a time, no personal attacks.
- Guide them through the method above and land on a concrete agreement.
- If it's beyond you (harassment, safety, repeated breakdowns), **escalate to a
  mentor** — that's not failure, it's judgment.

## Unit 5 — After the conflict

- Confirm the agreement and follow up that it's holding.
- Repair the relationship — a resolved conflict can leave people *closer* if
  handled with respect.
- Reflect on what caused it; sometimes the fix is a process change, not a person.

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Classify a recent disagreement as healthy or unhealthy and explain why.
- [ ] Walk through the resolution method on a real or role-played conflict.
- [ ] Mediate (or role-play mediating) a dispute between two teammates while
      staying neutral.
- [ ] State which situations you would escalate to a mentor instead of handling
      yourself.
