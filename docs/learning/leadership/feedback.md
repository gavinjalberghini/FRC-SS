## Overview

Teams improve only as fast as they exchange honest feedback. Done well, feedback
makes people better and strengthens trust; done badly, it breeds resentment or
gets avoided entirely. This lesson teaches a simple, respectful way to give
feedback and — just as important — how to receive it.

## Prerequisites

- [Communication Fundamentals](../communication-fundamentals/).

## What you'll learn

- A structure for giving clear, actionable feedback.
- How to give praise that actually reinforces good work.
- How to receive feedback without getting defensive.

## Unit 1 — What good feedback is

Good feedback is:

- **Specific** — about one concrete thing, not a vague vibe.
- **Timely** — close to when it happened, not saved up for months.
- **Actionable** — the person can do something different next time.
- **About behavior, not identity** — "the wires weren't strain-relieved" not
  "you're sloppy."

## Unit 2 — A structure: Situation → Behavior → Impact

A reliable format (SBI):

1. **Situation** — "In yesterday's build meeting…"
2. **Behavior** — "…you committed to the bracket but didn't mention you were
   blocked…"
3. **Impact** — "…so we lost a day waiting on it."

Then **discuss the fix** together. SBI keeps feedback factual and avoids
character attacks. Use the same structure for praise — specific praise ("your
labeling made debugging fast") reinforces the exact behavior you want repeated.

## Unit 3 — Receiving feedback

This is the harder half. When you get feedback:

- **Assume good intent** and listen fully before responding.
- **Don't defend or explain reflexively** — sit with it first.
- **Ask for specifics** if it's vague: "Can you give me an example?"
- **Say thank you.** Someone took a risk to help you improve.
- **Decide what to act on.** You don't have to agree with all of it, but look for
  the kernel of truth.

How you receive feedback determines whether people keep giving it to you.

## Unit 4 — Building a feedback habit

- Make it **routine**, not a big scary event (quick retrospectives after a
  project).
- **Default to private** for corrective feedback, public for praise.
- Ask for feedback on yourself regularly — it models the behavior and builds
  safety.

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Give a piece of corrective feedback using the SBI structure.
- [ ] Give specific praise that names the exact behavior.
- [ ] Receive a piece of feedback without defending, ask a clarifying question,
      and thank the giver.
- [ ] Run a short retrospective with your group (what went well / what to change).
