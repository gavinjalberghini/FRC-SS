## Overview

Senior leaders are paid — in trust — to make decisions, often with incomplete
information and a ticking clock. Decide too slowly and the team stalls; decide
recklessly and you lose credibility. This lesson covers how to make good
decisions, involve the right people, and solve problems at their root.

## Prerequisites

- All Emerging Leader and Team Leader lessons.

## What you'll learn

- A structured approach to decisions and problem-solving.
- How to choose the right level of involvement for a decision.
- How to decide under uncertainty and time pressure.

## Unit 1 — A decision process

1. **Frame the real question.** What exactly are we deciding, and by when?
2. **Gather just enough information** — perfect information rarely exists; don't
   let analysis become avoidance.
3. **Generate options** — at least two or three; "do nothing" is an option too.
4. **Weigh against criteria** — cost, time, risk, alignment with the goal.
5. **Decide and commit** — make the call clearly.
6. **Communicate it** — what was decided, why, and who's affected.
7. **Review the outcome** — did it work? Learn for next time.

## Unit 2 — Solve the root problem

For problem-solving, don't fix symptoms:

- Ask **"why" repeatedly** (the "5 Whys") to reach the root cause.
- Distinguish a **one-time fix** from a **process change** that prevents
  recurrence.
- Connect with the disciplined troubleshooting mindset from the technical tracks —
  isolate, test, and verify.

## Unit 3 — How much to involve others

Match involvement to the decision:

- **Decide alone** for small, reversible, low-stakes calls — speed matters.
- **Consult** key people for input, then decide, for medium-stakes calls.
- **Build consensus** for big, hard-to-reverse decisions that need buy-in.

A useful test: **reversible decisions** can be made fast and cheap; **one-way
doors** deserve more care and more voices.

## Unit 4 — Deciding under uncertainty

- **Don't wait for certainty** that won't come; decide with the best available
  information.
- Weigh the **cost of delay** against the cost of being wrong.
- Prefer **reversible experiments** — try something small, learn, adjust.
- Own the outcome either way; a leader who blames the team after a bad call loses
  trust fast.

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Walk a real decision through the 7-step process.
- [ ] Use the 5 Whys to find the root cause of a recurring problem.
- [ ] Classify three decisions by how much team involvement each needs.
- [ ] Identify whether a decision is reversible, and set your pace accordingly.
