## Overview

A robot is held together by hundreds of fasteners. Choosing the right one — and
installing it correctly — is the difference between a mechanism that survives a
season and one that rattles apart in a match. This lesson teaches the common
fasteners, how threads are sized, and where each type is used.

## Prerequisites

- [Shop Safety & PPE](../shop-safety/) and [Measurement & Layout](../measurement-layout/).

## What you'll learn

- The common fastener families and their drive types.
- How thread sizes are specified (imperial and metric).
- When to use bolts vs. rivets vs. screws, and how to keep them from loosening.

## Unit 1 — Fastener families

- **Socket head cap screws (SHCS)** — the FRC workhorse, driven with a hex (Allen)
  key. Strong and compact.
- **Button head screws** — lower profile, less strong; good where clearance
  matters.
- **Flat head screws** — countersink flush into a part.
- **Nuts** — plain, **nylon-lock (nylock)** which resist vibration, and pressed-in
  **nutserts/PEM nuts** for thin material.
- **Rivets** — permanent fasteners installed with a rivet tool (their own lesson).
- **Standoffs and spacers** — set spacing between plates.

## Unit 2 — Thread sizes

Threads are specified by **diameter** and **pitch**:

- **Imperial** (common in FRC): e.g. **#10-32** means a #10 diameter with 32
  threads per inch; **1/4-20** means 1/4 in diameter, 20 TPI. The most common FRC
  sizes are **#10-32** and **1/4-20**.
- **Metric**: e.g. **M3 × 0.5** means 3 mm diameter, 0.5 mm pitch.

A bolt and the nut/tapped hole must match **both** diameter and pitch. A thread
gauge or the markings on a vendor bin tell you the size.

## Unit 3 — Using fasteners well

- **Don't strip the drive.** Seat the hex key/bit fully and use the correct size.
- **Lock against vibration:** use nylock nuts, or a thread locker where parts must
  not back out (follow the type the build calls for).
- **Grip length matters:** the bolt should engage enough threads (roughly its own
  diameter of engagement) without bottoming out.
- **Don't overtighten** into aluminum — you can strip soft threads. Snug, then a
  controlled final turn.

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Identify SHCS, button head, flat head, nylock nut, and a rivet on sight.
- [ ] Read a thread callout like #10-32 and 1/4-20 and state what each part means.
- [ ] Pick an appropriate fastener and locking method for a sample joint.
- [ ] Drive a screw with the correct hex key without stripping it.
