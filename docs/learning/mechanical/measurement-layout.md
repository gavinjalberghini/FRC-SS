## Overview

"Measure twice, cut once" is the oldest rule in the shop because material is
expensive and a bad cut can't be undone. Accurate measurement and clear layout
are the foundation of every part you'll make. This lesson teaches you to read the
common measuring tools and transfer measurements onto stock as crisp, accurate
marks.

## Prerequisites

- [Shop Safety & PPE](../shop-safety/).

## What you'll learn

- How to read a tape measure down to 1/16 in (and finer).
- How to read dial or digital calipers in decimal inches.
- How to convert between fractions and decimals.
- How to lay out and mark accurate cut and hole lines.

## Unit 1 — The tape measure

- The big numbers are inches; the marks between them are fractions: the longest
  in-between mark is **1/2**, then **1/4**, **1/8**, and the smallest are
  **1/16**.
- Read to the nearest mark, and note whether you need a finer tool.
- The hook on the end **floats** on purpose (by the thickness of the hook) so it
  reads correctly whether you push or pull against an edge — don't try to
  "fix" it.

## Unit 2 — Calipers

For precision work (hole spacing, shaft and bearing diameters, material
thickness), use **dial or digital calipers**, which read in **decimal inches**
(e.g. 0.500 in) or millimeters.

- **Outside jaws** measure outside dimensions (a tube's width, a shaft).
- **Inside jaws** measure inside dimensions (a bore, a slot).
- **Depth rod** measures hole/pocket depth.
- Zero the calipers closed before measuring, and don't overtighten — light,
  consistent pressure gives a repeatable reading.

## Unit 3 — Fractions ↔ decimals

You'll constantly switch between the two. Memorize the common ones:

| Fraction | Decimal |
| --- | --- |
| 1/16 | 0.0625 |
| 1/8 | 0.125 |
| 3/16 | 0.1875 |
| 1/4 | 0.250 |
| 3/8 | 0.375 |
| 1/2 | 0.500 |

To convert any fraction, divide the top by the bottom (3 ÷ 8 = 0.375).

## Unit 4 — Layout & marking

- Use a **combination square** to draw lines square (90°) to an edge, and to set
  a consistent distance from an edge.
- Mark with a fine **scribe**, **soapstone**, or sharp pencil/marker — a thick
  line is an inaccurate line. Cut to the **waste side** of the line.
- For hole centers, mark the intersection of two lines and **center-punch** it so
  the drill bit can't wander (covered in Drilling & Tapping).
- Account for **blade kerf** (the width of material a saw removes) when laying out
  multiple parts from one piece of stock.

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Read several lengths on a tape measure to the nearest 1/16 in.
- [ ] Measure a shaft, a bore, and a thickness with calipers in decimal inches.
- [ ] Convert three fractions to decimals and back without a chart.
- [ ] Lay out a part with square lines and center-punched hole locations,
      accounting for kerf.

## Notes

- When a drawing gives a dimension with a tolerance (e.g. 2.000 ± 0.005), the
  decimal places tell you how precise to be — that's a calipers job, not a tape
  job.
