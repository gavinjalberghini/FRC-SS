## Overview

Knowing your materials tells you how to cut them, how strong they'll be, and how
much they weigh. FRC robots are built from a fairly small palette of stock
shapes and materials. This lesson introduces the common ones so you can read a
bill of materials, pull the right stock, and understand why a designer chose it.

## Prerequisites

- [Shop Safety & PPE](../shop-safety/) and [Measurement & Layout](../measurement-layout/).

## What you'll learn

- The common materials and stock shapes used on FRC robots.
- How material choice affects strength, weight, and machinability.
- The difference between raw stock and COTS (commercial off-the-shelf) parts.

## Unit 1 — Aluminum

Aluminum is the dominant FRC structural material: strong, light, and easy to
machine. You'll see it as:

- **Box tube** — hollow rectangular tube (commonly **1×1 in** and **2×1 in**) with
  a standard hole pattern. The backbone of most frames and arms.
- **Angle, channel, and bar** — other extruded shapes for brackets and supports.
- **Plate/sheet** — flat stock for gussets, gearbox plates, and brackets,
  available in thicknesses like 1/16, 1/8, 1/4 in.

Common alloys are **6061** (general structure) and **7075** (stronger, used where
loads are high).

## Unit 2 — Plastics

- **Polycarbonate (Lexan)** — tough, nearly unbreakable clear plastic for guards,
  bellypans, and intake parts. Cuts and drills easily but can crack if scored at
  an edge.
- **HDPE / UHMW** — slippery plastics great for low-friction surfaces and wear
  parts.
- **Delrin (acetal)** — rigid, machinable plastic for rollers, spacers, and small
  parts.

## Unit 3 — Stock vs. COTS

- **Raw stock** is material you cut and machine yourself (tube, plate, plastic).
- **COTS parts** are bought ready-made: gears, sprockets, pulleys, bearings,
  wheels, shafts, and gearboxes. Using COTS where possible saves build time and
  improves reliability.

Match the tool and feeds to the material — aluminum, polycarbonate, and steel all
cut differently (covered in the cutting and drilling lessons).

## Steps & acceptance criteria

- [ ] Identify 1×1 and 2×1 aluminum box tube, plate, and polycarbonate on sight.
- [ ] State a typical use for each common material.
- [ ] Explain the difference between raw stock and a COTS part with examples.
- [ ] Pull the correct stock for a simple part from a bill of materials.
